Orange Cats Popular with Viking Invaders

Feline genetics demostrate that around 1000AD Vikings developed a love for orange cats which they found near the Black Sea. Viking outposts in Iceland, Scotland, and the Faroe Islands are home to unusually large numbers of these cats. No one knows why the Vikings were so taken with orange cats. They are neither especially good mousers nor particularly adapted well for living at sea on ships.

Orange Cat Quick Facts

80% of orange cats are male do to a chromosome mutation on the "X" sex-linked chromosome. This mutation is recessive and requires two mutant "X" chromosomes in female cats, but any male cat with a mutant "X" chromosome is expressed as orange. The orange color is caused my the presence of "pheomelanin" which is produced by mutatations in the melanin producing genes. They are also called Ginger or Marmalade cats.

The Strange Case of Toxoplasmosis Gondii

Toxoplasmosis is the reason pregnant women should not clean litter boxes. It can cause severe birth defects if the mother contracts it while pregnant. It is not a bacteria or virus, but actually an "obligate intracellular parasite." In humans it often migrates to the eye and is the number one cause of retinochoroiditis and accompanying retinal scars. Although only 3% of people infected have ocular manifestations, one can lose a significant amount of vision if that scar happens to be on a critical part of the retina, such as the macula.

A really wierd thing is when mice are infected it affects their brains, and their behaviour changes. Cats are not adversely effected. Mice, on the other hand become less risk adverse. Their reflexes are slowed, working memory is impaired, and they develop an attraction to the odor of cat urine. Cats conveniently find mice coming to them, and the cylcle repeats.

In humans it also affects behavior. These effects take place as quickly as three weeks after infection, and persist even after the paracyte has been eliminated. Reflexes are slowed and multiple cats are often aquired. Additional studies could confirm if crazy cat ladies usually test positive. Male motorcyclists are disproportionately affected. In a perverse twist, motorists of either sex who have T. gondii are three to four times more likely to die in car accidents, either from their increased disregard of the speed limit, or because the parasite wears down reaction times.

Chimpanzees infected with Toxoplasmosis develop a morbid attraction to leopard urine, following the cat and mouse pattern. It's not a happy ending for the Chimpanzees.

A visionary leader in Toxoplasmosis research is Jaroslav Flegr, an evolutionary biologist at Charles University in the Czech Republic, who asserts "Toxoplasma might even kill as many people as malaria, or at least a million people a year, " because of how it appears to change our response to dangerous situations. While no evidence exists that cats genetically engineered this biological mind-control agent, it does seem suspiciously convenient.
